Birds use a military-style sentry system to protect members of a group and improve the chances of a good meal, say researchers.

A team from Bristol University found that while groups of birds are foraging for food, one of them takes the role of sentry.

It informs its companions of any danger with a distinctive  "watchman's song".

The study, published yesterday in the journal Current Biology, likened the behaviour to soldiers on duty in hostile territory, who keep in regular radio contact with their colleagues to assure them all is well.

Natural selection suggests individuals should act selfishly to survive so this is a rare example of co-opearative behaviour in the animal world, th estudy said.

Researcher Dr Andy Radford said the birds studied  -  African pied babblers  -  use the "sentry" method to allow foraging birds to collect more food without disturbance and avoid predators.

He said: "The unselfish behaviour of the sentry is rewarded further down the line by the improved survival of individuals, which in turn leads to a larger group size.

"This increases the sentinel's chances of survival when the group is under attack or having to repel rivals from the territory.

"These exciting results are a great example of true co-operation. It's a win-win scenario."

The pied babbler is found in southern Africa and lives in groups of six to seven individuals.

The bird typically preys on scorpions and small snakes found beneath the surface of the ground.

The study of groups in the Kalahari Desert showed the foragers responded to the watchman's alarm calls alone, whether or not they could see it sitting in a tree.

The calls allow the group to continue-foraging without looking for the presence of the sentry, which increases foraging time and improves the group's survival rate.

In response to playbacks of recordings of the call, the foraging individuals spent less time looking out for predators, looked up less often, spread out more widely and spent more time out in the open.

Dr Radford said: "Decision-making in response to vocal cues is an important behaviour in social birds, and by studying it we can discover much about the way that different groups of animals develop language use.

"We are now investigating whether sentinels differ in their reliability and how this might influence the behaviour of their group-mates."

The £300,000 study was funded by the Biotechnology and Biological Sciences Research Council.
