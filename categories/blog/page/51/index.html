Young Koreans go back in time to embrace sweet, slightly fizzy taste of makgeolli

blog
Makgeolli was something your grandparents would drink. Known colloquially as nonju, or “farmer’s alcohol”, the fermented rice beverage had once been Korea’s most popular tipple, but by the 1990s it had lost ground as prosperity convinced drinkers to turn to more fashionable imports such as beer, wine and whisky. Things have come full circle. Young Koreans have embraced the sweet, slightly fizzy makgeolli, which is made by mixing rice with nuruk, a traditional fermentation starter derived from rice and wheat.
